# Chapter 1: Do positive interactions among plants matter ? Ragan Callaway

## Positive interactions and productivity

- Lot of studies examined the link between biomass and facilitation BUT...
- Only one examined the link between facilitation and productivity

Biomass production is a quantity of dry matter at a given time while productivity is a
rate of biomass production by unit of time. Percent solar efficiency is the
productivity rate by quantity of solar energy input (the idea beyond is that
plant under the canopy receive less solar input). 

- If we order facilitative effect by % amelioration compare to open area:  
  1. Solar efficiency (+3300%)
  2. Productivity (+830%)
  3. Biomass (+40%)

Generally, we assume that biomass at the end of the vegetative period is the
reliable measure of productivity.
But we see here that they are disconnected. Callaway argue that it might be due
to a important turnover of species during the growing season. If this effect is
common, we might be underestimate facilitative effect.

It adds that this effect is more important with one facilitator species, in
which we find competition with the average biomass on two years and facilitation
with the seasonal productivity.

## Positive interactions and diversity-ecosystem function

- Classic view of div-ES relationship: complementary. More complementary species
can exploit more resources and are less in competition due to difference in
niche.

- Facilitation could play a role by maintaining species diversity for example by
the indirect facilitation among the competitors.  

- Cardinale *et al.* (2002): link facilitation-productivity. The diversity of
  plants decrease more the stream flow than the plant monoculture, thus,
improving the resource comsumption of the invertebrates. 

- Spehn (2002): legumes facilitate forbs and grass.
The nitrogen transfer from the first to the seconds was strongly species
specific. This facilitative effect resulted in aboveground biomass increase.
This effect shows that facilitation may contribute to this facilitative effect.

- The BEF theory places redundancy hypothesis.
But may be that the "redundant species" in nonstressful conditions, can maintain
themselves or maintain others in more stressed conditions thanks to
facilitation. Hypothesis that redundant species in one condition could be
non-redundant in their reaction to stress. 

## Facilitation and exotic invasions

- Facilitation can increase the invasion potential of a non-native species.

- Maron & Connors (1996): found that dead lupin patches (*Lupinus*, legum) facilitate
more the non native, weedy species. Its effect is reduced of 50%  after 25
years, suggesting that facilitative effect can remain long after death.

- *Orbea* seeds beneficiate from shading of native shrub because their seeds
  grow less under temperature superior to 30°C.

## Facilitation and evolution in plant communities 

The current view of communities is the individualistic one (Gleason vs
Clement's superorganism), meaning that communities are not formed strongly
co-evolving species, their properties are not resulting from plant-plant
interactions.

But some studies seems to show there are evolutionary relationships in plant
communities and that facilitation plays a role in those relationships.

- Niche construction (Laland et al., 1996, 1999): environmental modification by
  organisms can alter the evolutionary trajectory of species. Facilitation
  mechanisms are often reported as environment modification.   

- Conservation guilds (Atsaat & ): defense guilds against hervivores composed of
  species with chemically different compounds could be an important mechanism in
  natural communities by preventing herbivores to specialized on one plant and so,
  to be highly virulent. 

- Evolutionary constraints: *Thymus* & *Bromus*. Some *Thymus* produce phenolic
  compounds. *Bromus* populations growing under the canopy of the phenolic
  producers *Thymus* populations are adapted to those compounds.

- Kidkidze & Callaway (2009): Competition based theory provide consistant
  explanation for speciation but some major evolutionary transitions are best
  explained by theory including positive interactions. Exemples include uni to
  multicellular life, emergence of cells and eusocial populations. 


# Chapter 2: Plant Interaction Indices Based on Experimental Plant Performance Data

Authors: Kikvidze and Armas

## Introduction

Interactions between 2 species classified according to their reciprocal effects on each
other. Neutralism does not necessary imply that the 2 species do not interact
but rather their negative and positive interactions compensate each other.

Many experimental studies focused on biomass accumulation relative to those
focusing on fecundity and survival.

- Single species experiment: use the focal as a phytometer. It is so use to
  evaluate the importance of different variables on plant growth. It can also be
  used as a control to evaluate plant interactions and is essential to assess
  the importance of interactions.

- Removal experiments: working with triplets of target individuals. One is
  marked as control, one whose the neighbors are removed and one which is
  harvested and dried to get the initial biomass. Each triplet means one
  replicate. The individuals belonging to one triplet are thought to be in the
  same micro-environment but enough far to not be in interaction.

## Measuring effects

The significance of the experimental should be test with raw data and standard
test (t-tests, ANOVA). The intensity of interaction will be measured with
interaction indices.

### Measure biomass gain

- Absolute Growth Rate: $AGR = (M_t - M_0)/t_exp$

Mass gain by unit of time. This form has one problem. The $M_0$ can vary from
light to heavy and won't change change the AGR. But for a given absolute growth rate, we can state a
plant begining from smaller size performs better that a individual beginning
heavier. For example, a plant performed better if it went from 10 to 100g than
if it went from 100 to 190g. To override this effect, people generally use
another index: the RGR.

- Relative Growth Rate: $RGR = (ln(M_t) - ln(M_0))/t_exp$

One reason for facilitation being overlooked was also due to the first
experimental setups. Interactions between were tested in controlled conditions,
benign, suitable for dominance of competitive interactions. (I am not sure that
I agree with this part).

### Measuring interactions

- It exists several indices of plant interactions. Few of them have good
  mathematical properties. The first index developed were intensity indices.

- difference between intensity and importance: Because relative importance of
  the effects of environment and neighbors vary along stress gradients. Brooker
*et al.* (2005) proposed the importance index which are normalized by the
performance of the target species in the more benign environment.

NB: Index of Brooker *et al.* (2005) has been modified by Seifan et al. (2010)
and more recently by Diaz-Sierra et al. (2016).

- Two majors criticisms about those indices:
  1. Mathematical properties desirables are often lacking 
  2. Indices do not provide separation between intra and inter competition.

### Initial biomass

There is a bias of not using initial biomass in intensity index. This bias is
proportional to the difference between final and initial biomass. If this
difference is very important, there is less problem.  But the bias of not using
initial biomass depends also on the true effect. It is more problematic because
it means your index will be not able to measure positive as good as negative
interactions.

However, the importance index do not rely on initial biomass and so, seems to be
free of this bias.

RGR produces negative results when the growth is very low, so it can't be used
in this situation for the interaction index and we have to use AGR instead.

# Chapter 4: Biotic interactions, biodiversity and community productivity

Authors: Richard Michalet and Blaise Touzard

## Introduction

- BEF: stability, productivity, invasibility
- Which biodiversity component drives the BEF
- Biotic are considered as an important determinant of community composition and
  richness
- Therefore, biotic interactions should be linked to ecosystem function

## Biotic interactions and biodiversity

- Competition was considered as the only driver of species richness
- Grime (1973) modeled the humped back relationship between species richness and
  environmental gradients (disturbance and stress)
- Competition was proposed to be maximal at the starting of the gradient and
  decrease linearly along the gradient letting succession from competitive to
  stress-tolerator (stress gradient) or ruderal species (disturbance gradient).
- Diversity is maximal at the intermediate pressure where can coexist the two
  strategies
- The pattern was also found in diversity-productivity relationship. But this
  pattern seems to be more common in studies which use biomass as a proxy for productivity and
  it should be noted that it was also the proxy used by Grime.
- Matter of scale: this relationship was not found at a scale larger than a
  continent. Metacommunity model can produce the same pattern at regional scale
  with stochastic factors. 
- Regional pools drive local richness.
- Michalet et al. (2002) however found support for this hypothesis in a alp
  altitudinal gradient.
- But high competition does not always lead to competitive exclusion
- Michalet et al. (2006) revisited the hump-backed relationship with the
  inclusion of facilitation.
- According to SGH, the importance of facilitation should be maximal at the
  intermediate position in the gradient.

## Biodiversity and ecosystem function: the role of biotic interaction

- Niche complementarity and facilitation were the two main biotic processes
  proposed to explain the BEF
- Niche complementarity: diverse assemblages contain species which exploit
  different resources and so, use more resources of the ecosystems and so, are
  more productive.
- But needed to separate the sampling effect for the complementarity effect: no
  consistent results 

- Facilitation: for example, the nitrogen brought by legumes (species specific
  effect) and also non-specific effects (see the redundancy effect in chap 1).

## Reconciling diversity experiments and natural patterns

- Inconsistency between natural patterns and experiment
- Experiment shows positive relationship between productivity and species
  richness
- Natural patterns show low diversity at the maximum productivity

- Loreau (2001) proposed that:
  - the correlation patterns observed between richness
  and productivity are driven by the environmental factors.
  - the experiment that the effect of species properties and diversity once
    removed the environmental factor

- Michalet's objections to Loreau (2001)'s proposition:
  1. in diversity experiment, diversity is supposed to increase productivity by
  niche complementarity (reduced competition). This effect is supposed to be
  constant all levels of natural productivity along environmental gradient.
  Contradiction with Grime (1974): competition is reduced in unproductive
  environments, how can richness decrease competition in this case ?
  
  2. The large scale natural patterns observed between productivity and
  richness is causal. Grime (1973) proposed that in most begnign environment,
  the competition should be the highest and so the richness decrease. So, the
  relationship between richness and productivity is causal and drive by the
  variation of competition intensity and importance along environmental gradients. 

- Matters of scale: the existence of different processes at different spatial
  scales have already been used in another inconstancy found between experiments
  and natural patterns. At the large scale, we find that there is a positive
  relationship between native richness and invasive species richness. While in
  experiment, we find that the native richness is inversely correlated to
  invasibility of the local community.
    - Processes proposed: in stressful conditions, facilitation permit to
      increase productivity, and not niche complementarity
    - Processes proposed: in benign conditions, the inconsistency is due to the
    use of biomass as a proxy for productivity. Original data from Touzard *et
    al.* shows that the productivity is maximal where the richness is minimal
    but we observe the inverse if we consider biomass instead of biomass + litter.
    - Niche complementarity: vertical evenness of the community increase with
      diversity and is thus proposed to increase productivity trough a reduced
      light competition.


# Chapter 6: Plant communities, plant-plant interactions and climate change 

Author: Rob W. Brooker 


## Plant-plant interactions and climate change

### Sources of information

1. Monitoring studies:

- pros: Long term studies, most persuasive indications of climate change

- cons: purely correlative

2. Experimental studies:

- pros: causal, variety of set up: Top Open Chambers (TOC), soil-warming cable,
  ..., variety of insights: ecosystem functionning, community composition

- cons: small scale plot: difficult to generalize.

3. Modelling studies: either mechanistic (abstract) or correlative (less
   abstract)

### Climate change and community composition

- Upward shift of alpine species and alpine tree line corresponding to T°C increases.

- Seed production: Enhanced temperatures increase viability of produced seeds.
- Seed dispersion: 
    - Change in migratory birds due to climate change which are vector of seed dispersal.
    - Land use change (increased fragmentation) can perturb species range shift.

- Species establishment: depend on abiotic conditions but also on **biotic**
  composition (pollinator and herbivores presence, ...)
    - Differences in ability to track climate change can let emerge previously
      unseen community composition.
    - Abiotic conditions change can release physiological limits and enhance
      plant growth, particularly for graminoids.
    - Southern species moving upwards could change the competitive relationship in
      communities, could result in species abundance change.
    - But competition for space could limit establishment of southern species
    - Phenology partitioning is an important aspect of coexistence. Climate
    warning will favor early flowering and growth. But Dunnet & Grime (1999) showed competition can
    prevent this adaptation. 
    - Facilitators could maintain maladapted species.

## Predicting plant community responses: considering interactions

Global change is considered as a major threat. Conservation biology is moving
forward with the aim to maintain biodiversity. It is necessary to understand the
impact of climate change on biodiversity.
